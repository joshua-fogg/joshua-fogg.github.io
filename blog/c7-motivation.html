Life Through A 'Pleasure, Passion and Purpose' Lens?

Chapter five introduces the pillars of happiness concept. Stating that happiness can be found in three forms, each of a varying sustainability.

The Three Pillars of Happiness:

  1. Pleasure:
    This type of happiness requires chasing the next high to sustain the elation.
    This type of happiness is considered short lived and invoked by a stimulus, which once removed causes the happiness to dissipate.
    Typically this seems to become the first point of call for most unhappy people, using it as a first point of call like with chocolate or commercial therapy for a pick me up or getting a bonus at work. It can be thought of as a consumable as it requires the most resource expenditure to sustain.
  2. Passion:
    Passion can also be known “flow,” where peak performance meets peak engagement, and time flies by.
    This is also known by yet another name more commonly so in athletic circles as "being in the zone."
    Sustaining this state of happiness is much more sustainable than pleasure, however it is harder to habituate it. This can be likened to a state of focus, where answers to obstacles can be more easily visualised and actuated.
  3. Higher Purpose:
    This is often refered to being part of something bigger than yourself that has meaning to you. Higher purpose has the highest sustainability as it facilitates altruistic sources, allows you to find a deep meaning and fulfillment in your life. Typically an altruistic based purpose or even shade tends to drive positive reinforcement from those around you. These aspects make this origin of happiness the most sustainable and resilient. This is considered where time and effort should be spent day to day. It is also highly dynamic and can vary greatly form person to person.


Taking this information into account the 'Three Pillars' reminds me more of a food pyramid, I must have been hungry when I read the chapter. Translating the Three Pillars into a pyramid you would want a resistant and long lasting foundation such as a Higher Purpose to nestle the remaining building blocks of Happiness on. The next tier would be a tier of Passion, before finally a comparatively brief amount of Passion.

I retrospect of reading the fifth chapter I can see how easily we try to use pleasure to compensate or evade a lack of higher purpose or passion in our day to day lives.
